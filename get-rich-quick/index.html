Today’s flavour: get rich quick schemes

This week has been a fruitful one when it comes to reporting on new types of spam emerging. Today we take a closer look at spam which promises to give you the ability to get rich quick, and with little to no effort.

The process

The email spam itself comes in many different shapes and sizes, there is no real pattern to it. When you click on the link in the email, you will be presented with a web page which looks something like this:

Right off the bat we can tell that the web page is not as professionally designed as some of the other ones which we’ve taken a look at on this blog. This may be indicative of different people being behind the different types of spam which we’re seeing.

Anatomy of the landing page

Starting from the top, the banner has your country’s flag on it in an effort to make it a more personalized feeling, as if you’ve been specially selected. Then, the real centrepiece of the landing page is the video which starts playing automatically. It features a man dressed in a suit and tie and driving around in a Ferrari, clearly meant to make an impression on the viewer. The man further emphasizes the personalized aspect of the whole thing by claiming that this webpage has been generated specifically for you in Canada, and that you cannot share it with anybody else.

The man in the video goes on to talk about how he owns a company and has several programmers working for him who came up with some software over the course of a year which automagically makes money and has something to do with stocks. The video leaves the details out intentionally.

The man goes on to show several fake bank balances of large sums of money in an effort to prove that the method he has invented really does work. After this, he sets up a scene with (clearly) paid actors to “try his method or he’ll give them $1000 cash upfront”.

The video goes on for another 30 minutes insisting that his method works and that the viewer must sign up quickly before there are no spots left. This ties together with the counters along the edges of the website which continue to update the “remaining free licenses” left as they go down.

An overarching theme of all of these scam webpages seems to be to create a sense of urgency in their victims.

Signing up

So, after watching theÂ short half an hour video and being left convinced that this is your key to being rich and famous, you decide to sign up using the form provided. What happens after that?

You are sent to a different website with a video of the same man playing. This time, the man tells you that you have only 7 minutes to deposit some money into your account because other people are quickly taking up the free licenses. Yet again the creation of a sense of urgency. Don’t think, just do.

Upon dismissing the video and proceeding to fund your account, you are presented with a dashboard to enter your credit card details, or alternatively pay with bitcoin.

Of course we had to see what happens when someone would go ahead and enter their credit card details into the form. With our handy no-balance virtual credit card provided by Squash Cash, we went ahead and filled out the form. However, unlike other scam sites before it, it didn’t even try to charge our credit card and instead simply showed an error message.

We tried several times and the credit card details were definitely correct, so we decided to move on and explore the rest of their website.

The scammer’s website

Along the top we can see a menu which consists of several tabs:

  1. My Account
  2. Trade Now
  3. Deposit
  4. Education
  5. Trading Tools
  6. Help
  7. About Us
  8. Contact Us

Starting from the left:

My account

This tab contains your basic info, along with the ability to change your password. On the far right however is a section where you can upload documents. It seems as though there are several required documents in order to use their website:

They require a copy of your passport or drivers license, proof of address such as a recent utility bill, and a copy of your credit card or banking info. Sounds pretty reasonable to me.

One interesting thing to note about the withdrawal section is that they claim to charge a $35 fee per withdrawal. In the off chance that they aren’t just running away with everyone’s money, this could be how they make money from people who join and quickly regret it and want to take their money back.

Trade now

The Trade Now tab is likely meant to be the place to go when you actually want to start trading some stocks and making some money. In the middle you have an interface which appears to promise the ability to buy currency at a certain price and sell it at another price. Along the right you have a leaderboard of sorts with the top dollar amounts earned by various (likely fake) users.

Deposit

This is the page we were placed on when we first got sent to the website and told to quickly deposit some money.

Education

On the education tab are videos on various different basic topics relating to economics, finance, and stocks along with some of their own secret strategies and an ebook too.

Trading tools

This tab showcases some supposedly recommended trading tools and calculators of different kinds to aid in your buying and selling of currencies.

Help

The Help section is pretty typical, just normal looking FAQs.

About US

The About Us section features a convincing certificate-looking page to put the viewer at ease that these guys are certified and legit.

Contact Us

The contact us web page shows various means of getting in contact with the so called support staff.

Chances are they have customized landing pages and videos for each of those countries on the map.

Leaderboards

The last interesting piece to this website is the leaderboard present on the side at all times. Upon clicking it, you are brought to the following page:

This interface appears to give the user the ability to simply copy the trades which the top dogs are doing, in an effort to earn a profit for themselves. The sole purpose of this leaderboard is to attempt to assure the viewer that there are in fact other people who are using this service and making money, further putting them at ease.

Extras

Upon signing up, I received an email at the address which I registered with:

It looked reasonably professional, and so I decided to go along with it and see what happens if I replied.

I replied back saying that there was an error processing my credit card details and if they could process it manually.

They replied back within a minute gladly obliging to processing my credit card for me.

To which I replied with my credit card details:

And I never heard from them again.

What’s interesting however is that they didn’t even attempt to charge my card. It’s because of this that I believe the real purpose of this whole setup is to claim credit card numbers, and ultimately sell them on underground markets.

If they didn’t want to sell the credit card numbers which they collect, then they should have charged mine as soon as I handed over the details. But instead they chose to ignore me.

Summary

This was possibly the most involved scam which I have covered on this blog. The people behind this went to considerable effort to make the website look somewhat believable, even if the initial landing page with the video was leaning on the mediocre side.

This is not the first time I have seen this type of get rich quick spam being blasted out, however it is the first time I’ve seen the avenue for credit card collection done like this.

Takeaways

  1. If something seems too good to be true, it usually is. Don’t fall for random strangers telling you that they can help you get rich quick if you give them money first.
  2. Be a good friend, and be sure to break the bad news if anybody enthusiastically sends you links to this sort of thing.
  3. Always be very selective about who you give your credit card details to. Definitely do not follow in my footsteps which I leave behind on this blog because the credit card I use for my investigations is special and money cannot be taken from it.

Until next time,

Stay safe.
