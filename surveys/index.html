Surveys

Posted onJanuary 2, 2017 Edit onJanuary 8, 2017 by Raz

Today’s flavour: surveys

A new flavour of spam has emerged lately with the apparent promise of being rewarded for filling out quick surveys.

The process

The spam email which you will get may take on any number of formats, with the content not necessarily explicitly mentioning anything about surveys. The emails which I got simply contained a link with the title “Hi <name>” in the body, and a subject line saying “<name> in for a big surprise! – Don’t miss”.

Upon clicking the link, there are several layers of redirection before finally landing at the survey page. The survey page itself will also take on different shapes and sizes, however the theme stays the same: you are presented with a survey to complete and the promise of a prize at the end.

One thing to note right off the bat is that these websites also appear to have a fake comments section at the bottom, something we have seen before with the miracle pill scam pages. The intention is likely the same; to generate a sense of confidence that the web page is legitimate. If other people are saying good things about it, then it must really be good.

The survey questions themselves differ based on the web page you happen to land on, but in the case of the fake Amazon survey, the questions were as follows:

  1. Gender
  2. How do you normally access Amazon?
  3. How often do you visit Amazon?
  4. How would you rate your satisfaction with the Amazon website?
  5. Do you follow Amazon on social media?

At which point it tells you that it is submitting your answers, then checking offer inventory, and finally it displays the promised prizes which are available for you.

The prizes differ from survey to survey, but in my instance I had anti-aging cream, male enhancement pills, muscle enhancement pills, wrinkle reduction cream, and one which was “out of stock”: a macbook pro.

I noticed that there is a comment textbox where you can post your own comment, and upon trying to do so you will simply get a popup which says your comment is “currently awaiting approval”.

If any of these pages seem familiar, it’s because they are; they have the identical look and feel to the checkout pages from the miracle pill scam. The pages are carefully crafted to look convincing and professional. In addition, they create a sense of urgency by displaying a notice at the top which indicates to the reader that the supply of the item is running low, and that they should order as soon as possible.

But wait, all of these pages are asking where to send the trial to. They aren’t asking you to buy anything, so what’s the big deal?

The end goal

Upon filling out the form for claiming your free trial, we finally get to the part of the process where we find out how the scammer makes money from all of this:

You have to enter your credit card details and pay for “shipping and handling“. I have a virtual credit card from Square Cash which has no balance and which I like to use for things like this to see what actually happens when you enter your credit card. In this case, they genuinely try to charge $7.99 from the merchant “Azd*buildxtreme”.

Summary

It’s pretty clear that the same people who are behind this spam were also behind the miracle pill spam which was sent out several weeks ago. The end goal is the same – to get you to give them your credit card info – but the method of obtaining it is slightly different.

The look and feel of the web pages used for the scam is quite convincing and high quality. It is not the traditional “slap anything together” type of web pages which normally accompany these sorts of things.

Takeaways

  1. As always, if something seems to good to be true, it usually is.
  2. If your friends send you links to surveys like these, let them know it’s fake.
  3. Be very selective of who you give your credit card details to.

Until next time,

Stay safe.
