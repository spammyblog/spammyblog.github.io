Bank loans

Posted onDecember 28, 2016 by Raz

Today’s flavour: Bank Loans

Quite often I would receive spam email in which the body of the email asks to reply back to the email, and I’ve never replied back to one of these until today. To my surprise, the person on the other side of the spam gets back to you within 24 hours.

The process

The first email received said that they are offering loans for 3% interest, and that I must reply if I’m interested.

Shortly after replying with “absolutely!”, I received an email which asked me to fill out a form with my name, amount needed, location, occupation, and some other information. It is worth noting that they advertise a range of loans from $5,000 all the way to $10,000,000 – quite impressive.

Some time goes by, and then the spammer gets back to me with more details about my $10,000 loan which has been approved! Great news! The last thing I need to give them is my banking information so they can give me the loan in the form of a direct deposit.

Upon filling out some fake banking information, several minutes later they get back to me telling me that it’s illegal for them to give out loans without having some insurance policy first. The policy costs a small fee of $250, and to let them know if I’m okay with that.

At this point we finally get to the part of the process where the spammer becomes a scammer. Upon receiving this email, it’s pretty clear what the next step will be: they want us to send them money before they send us money.

Finally they give me instructions to send them $250 via Money gram. This is where I had to stop, but we can take some pretty good guesses at what happens next.

Summary

This spam is sent out targeting people who are in need of a loan, and hopefully a little bit gullible. Upon baiting their victim in with promises of large loans with low interest rates and having them fill out multiple forms including their direct deposit information, they then get their victim to send them a (comparatively) small sum of money. Once the money is transferred to the scammers, they take that money and you never hear from them again. No loan for you.

Some key take-aways:

  1. Don’t trust random people claiming to want to give you money for free over email
  2. When in doubt, consult with friends and family
  3. As always, if something seems too good to be true, it probably is

Until next time,

Stay safe.
