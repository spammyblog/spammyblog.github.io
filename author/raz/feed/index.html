]]> Get rich quick https://spam-blog.razb.me/get-rich-quick/ Wed, 04 Jan 2017 04:28:32 +0000 https://spammy.blog/?p=186 Today’s flavour: get rich quick schemes

This week has been a fruitful one when it comes to reporting on new types of spam emerging. Today we take a closer look at spam which promises to give you the ability to get rich quick, and with little to no effort.

The process

The email spam itself comes in many different shapes and sizes, there is no real pattern to it. When you click on the link in the email, you will be presented with a web page which looks something like this:

Right off the bat we can tell that the web page is not as professionally designed as some of the other ones which we’ve taken a look at on this blog. This may be indicative of different people being behind the different types of spam which we’re seeing.

Anatomy of the landing page

Starting from the top, the banner has your country’s flag on it in an effort to make it a more personalized feeling, as if you’ve been specially selected. Then, the real centrepiece of the landing page is the video which starts playing automatically. It features a man dressed in a suit and tie and driving around in a Ferrari, clearly meant to make an impression on the viewer. The man further emphasizes the personalized aspect of the whole thing by claiming that this webpage has been generated specifically for you in Canada, and that you cannot share it with anybody else.

The man in the video goes on to talk about how he owns a company and has several programmers working for him who came up with some software over the course of a year which automagically makes money and has something to do with stocks. The video leaves the details out intentionally.

The man goes on to show several fake bank balances of large sums of money in an effort to prove that the method he has invented really does work. After this, he sets up a scene with (clearly) paid actors to “try his method or he’ll give them $1000 cash upfront”.

The video goes on for another 30 minutes insisting that his method works and that the viewer must sign up quickly before there are no spots left. This ties together with the counters along the edges of the website which continue to update the “remaining free licenses” left as they go down.

An overarching theme of all of these scam webpages seems to be to create a sense of urgency in their victims.

Signing up

So, after watching the short half an hour video and being left convinced that this is your key to being rich and famous, you decide to sign up using the form provided. What happens after that?

You are sent to a different website with a video of the same man playing. This time, the man tells you that you have only 7 minutes to deposit some money into your account because other people are quickly taking up the free licenses. Yet again the creation of a sense of urgency. Don’t think, just do.

Upon dismissing the video and proceeding to fund your account, you are presented with a dashboard to enter your credit card details, or alternatively pay with bitcoin.

Of course we had to see what happens when someone would go ahead and enter their credit card details into the form. With our handy no-balance virtual credit card provided by Squash Cash, we went ahead and filled out the form. However, unlike other scam sites before it, it didn’t even try to charge our credit card and instead simply showed an error message.

We tried several times and the credit card details were definitely correct, so we decided to move on and explore the rest of their website.

The scammer’s website

Along the top we can see a menu which consists of several tabs:

  1. My Account
  2. Trade Now
  3. Deposit
  4. Education
  5. Trading Tools
  6. Help
  7. About Us
  8. Contact Us

Starting from the left:

My account

This tab contains your basic info, along with the ability to change your password. On the far right however is a section where you can upload documents. It seems as though there are several required documents in order to use their website:

They require a copy of your passport or drivers license, proof of address such as a recent utility bill, and a copy of your credit card or banking info. Sounds pretty reasonable to me.

One interesting thing to note about the withdrawal section is that they claim to charge a $35 fee per withdrawal. In the off chance that they aren’t just running away with everyone’s money, this could be how they make money from people who join and quickly regret it and want to take their money back.

Trade now

The Trade Now tab is likely meant to be the place to go when you actually want to start trading some stocks and making some money. In the middle you have an interface which appears to promise the ability to buy currency at a certain price and sell it at another price. Along the right you have a leaderboard of sorts with the top dollar amounts earned by various (likely fake) users.

Deposit

This is the page we were placed on when we first got sent to the website and told to quickly deposit some money.

Education

On the education tab are videos on various different basic topics relating to economics, finance, and stocks along with some of their own secret strategies and an ebook too.

Trading tools

This tab showcases some supposedly recommended trading tools and calculators of different kinds to aid in your buying and selling of currencies.

Help

The Help section is pretty typical, just normal looking FAQs.

About US

The About Us section features a convincing certificate-looking page to put the viewer at ease that these guys are certified and legit.

Contact Us

The contact us web page shows various means of getting in contact with the so called support staff.

Chances are they have customized landing pages and videos for each of those countries on the map.

Leaderboards

The last interesting piece to this website is the leaderboard present on the side at all times. Upon clicking it, you are brought to the following page:

This interface appears to give the user the ability to simply copy the trades which the top dogs are doing, in an effort to earn a profit for themselves. The sole purpose of this leaderboard is to attempt to assure the viewer that there are in fact other people who are using this service and making money, further putting them at ease.

Extras

Upon signing up, I received an email at the address which I registered with:

It looked reasonably professional, and so I decided to go along with it and see what happens if I replied.

I replied back saying that there was an error processing my credit card details and if they could process it manually.

They replied back within a minute gladly obliging to processing my credit card for me.

To which I replied with my credit card details:

And I never heard from them again.

What’s interesting however is that they didn’t even attempt to charge my card. It’s because of this that I believe the real purpose of this whole setup is to claim credit card numbers, and ultimately sell them on underground markets.

If they didn’t want to sell the credit card numbers which they collect, then they should have charged mine as soon as I handed over the details. But instead they chose to ignore me.

Summary

This was possibly the most involved scam which I have covered on this blog. The people behind this went to considerable effort to make the website look somewhat believable, even if the initial landing page with the video was leaning on the mediocre side.

This is not the first time I have seen this type of get rich quick spam being blasted out, however it is the first time I’ve seen the avenue for credit card collection done like this.

Takeaways

  1. If something seems too good to be true, it usually is. Don’t fall for random strangers telling you that they can help you get rich quick if you give them money first.
  2. Be a good friend, and be sure to break the bad news if anybody enthusiastically sends you links to this sort of thing.
  3. Always be very selective about who you give your credit card details to. Definitely do not follow in my footsteps which I leave behind on this blog because the credit card I use for my investigations is special and money cannot be taken from it.

Until next time,

Stay safe.

]]> Surveys https://spam-blog.razb.me/surveys/ Tue, 03 Jan 2017 02:20:43 +0000 https://spammy.blog/?p=120 Today’s flavour: surveys

A new flavour of spam has emerged lately with the apparent promise of being rewarded for filling out quick surveys.

The process

The spam email which you will get may take on any number of formats, with the content not necessarily explicitly mentioning anything about surveys. The emails which I got simply contained a link with the title “Hi <name>” in the body, and a subject line saying “<name> in for a big surprise! – Don’t miss”.

Upon clicking the link, there are several layers of redirection before finally landing at the survey page. The survey page itself will also take on different shapes and sizes, however the theme stays the same: you are presented with a survey to complete and the promise of a prize at the end.

One thing to note right off the bat is that these websites also appear to have a fake comments section at the bottom, something we have seen before with the miracle pill scam pages. The intention is likely the same; to generate a sense of confidence that the web page is legitimate. If other people are saying good things about it, then it must really be good.

The survey questions themselves differ based on the web page you happen to land on, but in the case of the fake Amazon survey, the questions were as follows:

  1. Gender
  2. How do you normally access Amazon?
  3. How often do you visit Amazon?
  4. How would you rate your satisfaction with the Amazon website?
  5. Do you follow Amazon on social media?

At which point it tells you that it is submitting your answers, then checking offer inventory, and finally it displays the promised prizes which are available for you.

The prizes differ from survey to survey, but in my instance I had anti-aging cream, male enhancement pills, muscle enhancement pills, wrinkle reduction cream, and one which was “out of stock”: a macbook pro.

I noticed that there is a comment textbox where you can post your own comment, and upon trying to do so you will simply get a popup which says your comment is “currently awaiting approval”.

The prizes

So, what happens when you select a prize? Here are pictures of the webpages I was presented with:

Anti-aging cream

Male enhancement pill

This one’s not safe for work. Click here to see the website screenshot.

Muscle enhancement pill

Anti-wrinkle cream

If any of these pages seem familiar, it’s because they are; they have the identical look and feel to the checkout pages from the miracle pill scam. The pages are carefully crafted to look convincing and professional. In addition, they create a sense of urgency by displaying a notice at the top which indicates to the reader that the supply of the item is running low, and that they should order as soon as possible.

But wait, all of these pages are asking where to send the trial to. They aren’t asking you to buy anything, so what’s the big deal?

The end goal

Upon filling out the form for claiming your free trial, we finally get to the part of the process where we find out how the scammer makes money from all of this:

You have to enter your credit card details and pay for “shipping and handling“. I have a virtual credit card from Square Cash which has no balance and which I like to use for things like this to see what actually happens when you enter your credit card. In this case, they genuinely try to charge $7.99 from the merchant “Azd*buildxtreme”.

Summary

It’s pretty clear that the same people who are behind this spam were also behind the miracle pill spam which was sent out several weeks ago. The end goal is the same – to get you to give them your credit card info – but the method of obtaining it is slightly different.

The look and feel of the web pages used for the scam is quite convincing and high quality. It is not the traditional “slap anything together” type of web pages which normally accompany these sorts of things.

Takeaways

  1. As always, if something seems to good to be true, it usually is.
  2. If your friends send you links to surveys like these, let them know it’s fake.
  3. Be very selective of who you give your credit card details to.

Until next time,

Stay safe.

]]> Bank loans https://spam-blog.razb.me/bank-loans/ Wed, 28 Dec 2016 07:26:12 +0000 https://spammy.blog/?p=108 Today’s flavour: Bank Loans

Quite often I would receive spam email in which the body of the email asks to reply back to the email, and I’ve never replied back to one of these until today. To my surprise, the person on the other side of the spam gets back to you within 24 hours.

The process

The first email received said that they are offering loans for 3% interest, and that I must reply if I’m interested.

Shortly after replying with “absolutely!”, I received an email which asked me to fill out a form with my name, amount needed, location, occupation, and some other information. It is worth noting that they advertise a range of loans from $5,000 all the way to $10,000,000 – quite impressive.

Some time goes by, and then the spammer gets back to me with more details about my $10,000 loan which has been approved! Great news! The last thing I need to give them is my banking information so they can give me the loan in the form of a direct deposit.

Upon filling out some fake banking information, several minutes later they get back to me telling me that it’s illegal for them to give out loans without having some insurance policy first. The policy costs a small fee of $250, and to let them know if I’m okay with that.

At this point we finally get to the part of the process where the spammer becomes a scammer. Upon receiving this email, it’s pretty clear what the next step will be: they want us to send them money before they send us money.

Finally they give me instructions to send them $250 via Money gram. This is where I had to stop, but we can take some pretty good guesses at what happens next.

Summary

This spam is sent out targeting people who are in need of a loan, and hopefully a little bit gullible. Upon baiting their victim in with promises of large loans with low interest rates and having them fill out multiple forms including their direct deposit information, they then get their victim to send them a (comparatively) small sum of money. Once the money is transferred to the scammers, they take that money and you never hear from them again. No loan for you.

Some key take-aways:

  1. Don’t trust random people claiming to want to give you money for free over email
  2. When in doubt, consult with friends and family
  3. As always, if something seems too good to be true, it probably is

Until next time,

Stay safe.

]]> You could be hosting scams on your website and not even know it https://spam-blog.razb.me/you-could-be-hosting-scams-on-your-website-and-not-even-know-it/ Thu, 22 Dec 2016 05:23:49 +0000 https://spammy.blog/?p=81 The previous post I wrote was about the fake Miracle Pill spam sent out which tried to sell potential victims pills with super powers. These websites do not tend to stay live for very long as they get taken down (probably by law enforcement). However, upon looking back at some of the URLs which were sent to me from that flavour of spam, I noticed something interesting. The websites where the fake articles were being hosted were always subdomains, and not root domains (ie. they would be hosted at subdomain.example.com as opposed to just example.com). At first glance I assumed that the people behind the scam owned the whole domain and just happened to host their fake article on various subdomains of their choosing. However, upon visiting the root domains of these fake article websites, I noticed a disturbing trend. The root domains seem to belong to legitimate people and small businesses, completely unrelated to the miracle pill scam going on on their subdomains. So, what is going on here?

I did a whois lookup to find out some more information about the owners of these domains, and it seems as though they’re all registered with GoDaddy – a popular registrar which also offers web hosting and various other services.

This on its own isn’t suspicious, however, whois lookups also reveal the email address of the person who purchased the domain. I decided to take the email addresses of these people and look them up on haveibeenpwned.com – a website where you can go and find out if you have been victim to any data breaches of various websites – and to my surprise, every single email address that I checked was indeed a part of one or more data breaches.

First, some background on data breaches:

From time to time, websites which have registered users (such as Dropbox, or Tumblr, or MySpace, or Yahoo) get hacked and have their databases of user data dumped for the public to see. This means that information varying from usernames, passwords, dates of birth, IP addresses, and much more, is made available on the Internet.

So what does that have to do with anything? The issue with data breaches is that people tend to reuse the same password for various online services – for example, someone might use the same email and password combination to login to their Facebook account and their MySpace account. Then, when MySpace gets hacked and all of those usernames and passwords are leaked to the public, someone can now login to the victim’s Facebook account using the same username and password that they used on MySpace.

This is likely precisely what happened to the people who are hosting miracle pill scams on their subdomains. They had signed up with their email address and password for a service like MySpace, and then their credentials got leaked onto the Internet, and spammers took all of those leaked email addresses and passwords and tried to login to GoDaddy with them.

Upon successfully logging into an innocent person’s GoDaddy account, they modified the DNS records to add their own subdomain with which they ultimately went on to host miracle pill scams on, and then send out huge quantities of spam email linking to subdomains of otherwise completely legitimate and innocent websites.

In this way, the people behind the scams can protect themselves from potentially being identified by using someone else’s websites to host their scams. This is a very clever approach and something we all need to look out for.

Key takeaways:

Firstly, I encourage everyone to head over to haveibeenpwned.com and check whether their accounts have been victim to any public data breaches, and if so, change your passwords immediately.

Second, it is good practise to never reuse the same password for two different services, that way even if one of them gets hacked and has your username and password leaked, it cannot be used to hijack any of your other accounts with other potentially more important services. A good tool to help with this is 1Password.com

In addition to never using the same password twice, it is also important to add two-factor authentication to as many of your accounts as possible. This ensures that even if somebody does somehow figure out your password, they still can’t gain access to your account.

Lastly, if you believe you may have been victim to someone else hijacking your domain’s DNS management account, immediately change your password and verify that your DNS records are exactly as you expect them to be. Remove any records which you do not recognize, and if possible, add two-factor authentication for your account.
